# The Chosen Life: A Reflection

## About the Game

*The Chosen Life* is an interactive narrative game that follows a character through one of the most universal experiences a person can have: leaving the place they've always called home to begin a new life somewhere else. The player moves through the final night at their house, the journey to the airport, and the first uncertain moments of arrival in a new country. Along the way, they confront the emotional weight of the decision — hesitation, fear, self-doubt, and the quiet question that trails every migrant: *did I make the right choice?* The game is built in Twine for its branching narrative, with an embedded Bitsy game in the middle that lets the player walk through the physical spaces the character is leaving behind.

## Why This Story

I chose this topic because migration is one of the few experiences that is genuinely universal. People leave their homes for every reason imaginable — education, work, love, survival, the hope of something better, or simply the refusal to stay where they are. The specifics differ wildly, but the emotional architecture is the same: the last night, the packed bag, the door closing behind you, the terror of wondering whether you've chosen correctly. I wanted to make a game that sat inside that feeling rather than trying to explain it. There is no winning or losing in *The Chosen Life* — only walking through a decision that millions of people are making, quietly, every single day.

## What I Wanted the Player to Feel

I wanted the player to feel *everything at once*. Hesitation when they linger in a familiar room. Fear when they reach the airport. The small, specific grief of leaving people behind. And underneath all of it, the flickering question of whether this new place will ever feel like home. The goal wasn't to make players sad — it was to make them sit with uncertainty, the way real migrants do, and to recognize that choosing to move is not a single moment but a long negotiation with yourself.

## Why Twine and Bitsy Together

I combined these two tools deliberately. Twine is excellent for branching text and gave me the narrative backbone — the choices, the inner monologue, the slow unfolding of the story. But pure text felt incomplete for a story about physical space and leaving. Bitsy, with its pixel-art rooms and retro aesthetic, carries a particular nostalgia — it *looks* like memory. Embedding a small Bitsy game inside the Twine story lets the player actually *walk* through the rooms the character is saying goodbye to, before returning to the narrative. Digital storytelling and game-play, layered together.

## What I Learned

Bitsy humbled me. Drawing rooms was fine, but the moment I tried to add exits and entrances between rooms, the whole thing became a puzzle. It taught me that even the "simplest" tools carry real design decisions beneath their surface — and that small creative tools are rarely as small as they look. More broadly, I learned that the hardest part of making a game isn't the code. It's deciding what emotion you want the player to leave with, and then trusting the tools, clumsy as they sometimes are, to carry it.
